[synopsis]

It's 300 years in the future and Gideon Smith has access to over 2 billion outfits but today he doesn't like any of them. This crisis is confounded when he discovers that most of humanity has been enslaved by a sex crazed alien and the planet is on the brink of total ecological destruction.

What's even weirder is that nobody's noticed and nobody seems to care.

Inspired by and likely to appeal to fans of ‘Stark’, ‘This Other Eden’, ‘Super Sad True Love Story’, ‘The Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy’, ‘Brave new World’ and ‘1984’, Goodbye Eden is a high octane, assault on the senses, absurdly embarrassing, apocalyptic prediction about the end of the world.

If an alien civilisation wanted to enslave humanity - they’d probably do it like this.

[themes]

There are two types of governance - democracy and dictatorship. Democracy demands the active participation of each member of society. It demands that each member of society make the individual effort to learn about and work with others to hold their representatives to account and ensure they work for the interests of the society as a whole, not just the interests of the few.

As soon as these duties are neglected, subverted, or outlawed, dictatorship is the inevitable outcome and can take many forms. Whether it be totalitarian regimes, military juntas, aristocractic or financial elites, corporate monopolies or algorithmic control, the result is the loss of power and self-determination.

Goodbye Eden imagines a society whose ancestors neglected the democracy they took for granted. It explores the limits of what can be done to a society that has decided to stop looking at the world around it and focuses only on itself. It describes a scenario in which humanity has allowed itself to become enslaved without noticing because it was too short sighted, too narciccistic, and too unwilling to sacrifice pleasure for a future.

Gideon

Utopia demands only one thing. That we be the best, most confident, self-actualised, atom perfect, high-ranking versions of ourselves we can be. Gideon Smith is struggling. Struggling to pick a gender and stick to it - the waif-like femininity his genetics imposed on him sapping him of political credibility. Battling the mood stabilisers and neuro-enhancers that force happiness and self-confidence on a mind that longs for self-loathing and doubt. Fighting the muscle spasms and stomach cramps that keep him lurking in the shadows of his insecurities, a dull low pitched buzzing the soundtrack to his mind. Gideon gets trapped in the details. The pores of his skin great craters of imperfection. The achievements still locked. The profile never groomed to satisfaction. Gideon knows there’s something wrong with the world - but his ego insists he believe it is him.

Elle

Elle has no idea who she is or where she came from. She woke up four weeks ago with no memory of her past - but she doesn’t care about these details. She knows just one thing. One memory that couldn’t be erased. The planet is in grave danger, and all she cares about with freight train determination is saving it. She doesn’t care how coarse or untamed her hair is. She never notices her rugged jaw, her wide shoulders or the deep cracked skin on the heels of her feet. She’s comfortable in her skin, no matter how cracked it is - because she sees a bigger picture through a permanent frown and pursed lips that stand in judgement of a society too distracted to notice its impending demise. Elle will gasp her last breath before she gives up fighting for her planet.

Zen

Zen could be most accurately described as a three thousand year old Asian Karl Lagerfeld who enjoys nothing more than a zero gravity orgy and a fine Bordeaux. The centuries spent on Earth have influenced his dress sense immensely. He felt most comfortable in 18th Century England - the industrial revolution his greatest achievement, the knee length Breech his favourite attire. But like all his achievements, Zen never sought credit, preferring to lurk in the shadows, pulling the strings and loosening the corsets. To him, achievement is nothing - mere distraction from the elixir of indulgence.

Zen sees the potential in humanity. The power in harnessing its collective consciousness. In weaponising it. In selling it to the highest bidder.
